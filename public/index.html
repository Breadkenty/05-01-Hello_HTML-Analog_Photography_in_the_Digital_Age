Analog Photography
in the Digital Era

There’s a common misconception that analog photography is a thing of the past, but nothing could be further from the truth! I am here to tell you that analog photography is still alive and well today and I hope I can cover some information on how it is still relevant in the digital world.

To start let’s answer some of the most pressing questions about analog photography:

  • “Can you even get film today?”
  • “Who develops film anymore nowadays?”
  • “Why shoot film? Digital can do everything film can!?”

“Can you even get film today?”

Ahh yes... The most common question I get about analog photography, the lack of clarity on its availability of resources. Would you be surprised to know that most Walmart and CVS stores carry a pack of film? It’s true! Most of these places usually carry one or two brands of typical ISO 200, 400, and 800 film stocks. Have you ever seen those disposable cameras that people used back in the day? Those disposable cameras are cheap film cameras that have those film stocks I mentioned. Yep, that’s still a thing and you can find them at Walmart, usually in the aisle where you find memory cards and batteries.

The two film giants in today’s world usually come down to Kodak and Fuji and you can be damn sure that they still make film! Now the film stocks you would find in these local stores may seem to lack quality due to its appeal to regular consumers. However, outside of the retail, most online photography stores carry other film stocks that are professional-grade. Kodak is a really good example of a company that primarily thrives on selling film. And they make really good ones that I find are some of my favorites like Kodak BW Tri-X 400 film and Kodak Color Portra 400. Fun fact… Did you know Kodak supplies film for a lot of movies that are released today? Yep, there are still many movie directors that prefer to shoot film for their movies, some of them include directors like Quentin Tarantino (The Hateful Eight 2015), Christopher Nolan (Interstellar 2013), and Rian Johnson (Star Wars: The Last Jedi 2017).

So to surmise, yes film is still available and widely available on the online market. They are not as readily available as it used to be before, but they are around if you know where to look.

“Who develops film anymore nowadays?”

There are usually two options that are available when it comes to developing films:

  • Send them out to a lab
  • Develop them on your own

Let’s talk about labs… The availability is not the same as it used to be. With the rise of digital photography, most of these labs have lost their place in the world because to the average consumer, film photography has been replaced by iPhones and digital cameras. But that doesn’t mean they don’t exist. There are still many labs that are still developing film across the country and they are not going away anytime soon (We’ll discuss more when we get to the film vs digital part). However, keep in mind that most CVS’ still take films and process them. Although I don’t recommend it because of their quality of scans, more on that soon…

The biggest change in the delivery of photos in the digital age is that they are delivered in a digital format (usually by large email attachments). Receiving physical prints isn’t as common as they used to be mainly due to the paradigm shift of how photos are treated in the digital era. Most people keep their photos on their phones, computers, or hard drives. However, most if not all labs still deliver photos as prints. But the most common option is to receive them digitally. So it’s pretty common to send out film to get it processed than going to a local lab. That covers the first option, now to the next…

Developing your film... Although an uncommon practice, it is still done today. Chemicals, tools, and information are still available to the public on how to develop your film. If you’re like me, some people like to use enlargers to print on photo papers than using a digital printer. Which brings me to the biggest point on why people choose to send film out over developing your own…

Digitizing film is a whole beast on its own. It’s a very complicated realm to dive into and resources and information aren’t as easy to find. This would be a topic for another time but let’s simplify this point by saying:

  • Films are digitized using special film scanners.
  • Some scanners are extremely expensive — and we’re talking $10,000 and up. A good lab is determined by how good their scanners are and the expertise of the person using it.
  • Scanning films is a whole art on its own and it takes a lot of practice.

Due to these reasons, it’s usually easier to send films out to labs and let them take care of the scanning. Looking at how labs scan their film is a common practice in the analog community. The biggest challenge here is not about finding a lab that develops film, but rather finding a lab that can provide you with really good scans if you care about the quality and the look of your photos. Now let’s segue into the next point while we’re on the topic of scans…

“Why shoot film? Digital can do everything film can!?”

So by now, you’re probably wondering… “Kento, film looks like it’s a lot of work! Why shoot film over digital? Digital is so much more simple and less expensive!”

Ah... reader, that’s a very common question I get all the time. Let me ask you this…

  • Why listen to vinyl, when you have Spotify?
  • Why cook your nice 5-star meal, when you can just go to a 5-star restaurant?
  • Why drive a manual, when automatic is easier?
  • Why buy a book, if you have a Kindle?

If any of those questions apply to you, you might be thinking something along the lines of ‘experience’. Music enthusiasts listen to vinyl for the raw sound of the songs. For those who like to cook at home, it’s a whole new experience to share with someone else. Car enthusiasts drive a manual because it makes you feel like you’re ‘really’ driving by being connected to the car. People read a physical paper book for the tactile feeling of the pages. It’s all the same concept for analog photography. There’s a sense of wholesomeness in the process of going back to the bare basics of how photography works. The physical element of film is a unique experience and to some more challenging than its digital counterpart.

However, that is not all there is to shooting film, it has its benefits as well. Let me list a few of them:

  • Film makes you slow down and being mindful of taking photos, as you are limited by the number of photos you can take per film. Some common numbers as a reference:

    • 35mm: 27 or 36 images per roll
    • 120mm (medium format): 9 to 16 images per roll
    • 4x5 or 8x10 (large format) sheet film: 1 photo per sheet
    • Digital Nikon D800: 700+ images per 32GB CF card

  • This is drastically different in comparison to the hundreds of photos a digital camera can take and store on to a memory card. To some people, they find this to be a benefit.

  • Film has a very specific ‘look’ that is difficult to replicate from a digital camera. There’s a huge difference in the ‘look’ between two platforms. Digital is a medium of perfection. It’s very precise, sharp, and has a wider dynamic range of colors to work with. Film on the other hand is a medium of imperfection. It’s grainy, nostalgic, and raw. As a result, it provides a very unique ‘look’ that digital cameras have a hard time replicating because of its sense of ‘imperfectness’ and some people like that. Also unlike digital, film has a narrow dynamic range. However, this acts as a benefit because its ability to interpret shadows and colors feel more natural than digital. The film ‘look’ is so popular that there are a lot of digital filters to get that film look. People have been trying to perfect the ‘film look’ filter for the last decade that it’s become a market. There are lots of companies and individuals who create Adobe Lightroom presets to turn their digital photos into film look-a-likes. You can thank Instagram filters for this obsession!

  • Film for the longest time dominated the quality battle against digital. To understand this you have to comprehend the idea of film sizes and their impact on quality. Let’s break this down:

    • Digital cameras have sensors where the image is projected by the lens. Once projected, the image gets recorded ultimately turning it into a digital image. Film works the same way, except instead of a digital sensor film IS the sensor.

    • The bigger the film (or sensor) the higher the quality. This is because the larger the film, the more information it can store. This type of measurement is what we call megapixels. This is why large format cameras that take 4x5 film or 8x10 film are the highest quality cameras on the market. There is no digital equivalent of sensors that are as big as those sheet films. Even with medium format 120mm film, for digital cameras to have sensors that big, you are looking to spend at least $10,000 on a Hasselblad digital back camera. Most pro-sumer digital cameras you find on the market have sensors that are around the size of a 35mm film hence the lower quality. However digital camera technology has advanced throughout the years where their smaller sensors can record more information. There are limitations to this because the larger the megapixels, the more memory it requires. And this is usually capped by the current state of technological progress on memory card capacities.

    • Ironically, the older the camera, the higher the quality. This is because photographers used larger films that were clunky and difficult to use. Through out the years, they focused on making cameras smaller by sacrificing the quality of images. This meant that digital cameras focused on the idea of ‘compactness’. Companies have not focused not on the size of the sensors but rather making small and compact cameras that can store more photos. With film, this isn’t so much of an issue and it’s cheaper than spending $10,000+ on a camera that can do so. But this is changing with how technology is progressing today.

There are more reasons for the benefits of shooting film but those are some major points to why people still shoot them today. Analog photography is everywhere, you just don’t realize it. Next time when you think of works by Richard Avedon for Vogue, Annie Leibovitz for The Rolling Stones, Cassandra Klos for Time, Steve McCurry for National Geographic, Ansel Adams and Clyde Butcher for their influential landscape photography, or even most elite photographers in the wedding industry all shot film as their medium.
